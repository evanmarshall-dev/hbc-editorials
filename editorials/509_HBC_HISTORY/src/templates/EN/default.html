HBC History

1670

The Royal Charter, granted by King Charles II on May 1670, served as HBC's original articles of incorporation and gave the Governor and Company of Adventurers of England trading into Hudson Bay exclusive trading rights over the entire Hudson Bay drainage basin - naming and claiming this territory Rupert's Land in honour of Prince Rupert, the king's cousin and first HBC Governor.

For its first 100 years, HBC conducted its business along the shores of Hudson Bay and adapted to the conditions and systems established by Indigenous Peoples. Survival and success in the fur trade depended on the knowledge and labour of Indigenous men and women.

1774

HBC builds Cumberland House, its first inland post.

Competition forced HBC to expand into the interior and build posts along the great river networks of the northwest, transforming the political, economic, and intimate lives of Indigenous Peoples.

1779

Heavily in debt to a Montreal fur trade entrepreneur, Germain Maugenest, an independent fur trader, joined HBC in 1779. His knowledge of the trade was seen as invaluable, and he was sent to England to meet with HBC's Board at Hudson's Bay House in London. There, he delivered his “Proposals of the Terms” under which he would enter into Hudson's Bay Company's service. He offered several suggestions for improving the growing inland trade from Fort Albany along the west coast of James Bay. Among his terms was a suggestion that the Company should regularly stock and trade “pointed” blankets.

1821

In 1821, HBC merged with its biggest rival, the Montreal-based North West Company. The resulting commercial enterprise now spanned the continent — all the way to the Pacific Northwest (modern-day Oregon, Washington, and British Columbia) and the North (Alaska, the Yukon, Northwest Territories and Nunavut).

1869

Two hundred years after the Royal Charter was granted, HBC reached an agreement with the British Crown to transfer Rupert's Land to the newly formed Dominion of Canada. This transaction known as the Deed of Surrender was concluded, much like the Royal Charter, without consulting Indigenous Peoples.

1881

Western settlement and the Gold Rush quickly introduced a new type of client to HBC — one that shopped with cash. The Company saw an opportunity and shifted its focus to retail to meet the demand for goods from the influx of settlers. It transformed trading posts into sales shops, stocked with a wider variety of goods than ever before. The Company continued to grow and adapt as more families moved from rural to urban areas, and department stores became downtown destinations.

In 1881, HBC opened a modest dry goods, grocery, and hardware store at the corner of Winnipeg's Main and York streets.

1907

The growth of retail encouraged HBC to explore a wide variety of commercial pursuits. As of 1907, a thriving wholesale business featuring liquor, canned salmon, coffee, tea, and tobacco supplemented fur and retail pursuits.

Shipping and natural resources, particularly oil and gas, were other important opportunities as HBC evolved in the first half of the 20th century.

1910

HBC restructures its business into three departments: Fur Trade, Land Sales and Retailing.

1912

In 1912, the Company began an aggressive modernization program, and although delayed by the outbreak of the First World War, resulted in the “original six” HBC department stores.

HBC Board names the Canadian Advisory Committee to advise on overseas growth. In 1930, the Canadian Committee's powers are extended to oversee all day-to-day operations of the Company in Canada.

1920

In 1920, Hudson's Bay Company launched its first corporate newsletter, The Beaver, in celebration of its 250th anniversary. It was originally subtitled “A Journal of Progress” and primarily dealt with items of interest to those associated with HBC. The journal's mission was to promote pride, loyalty, and camaraderie, especially among city-based retail employees. All associates, from stenographers to ship captains, were invited to contribute personal news items, histories, biographies or other notes
